In the few months since we began our Cyber Safety Awareness initiative, many families have approached us seeking guidance after recognizing signs and situations similar to those discussed in our awareness sessions. One such case left a lasting impression on our team and highlighted how digital dependency can quietly affect a young person's life.

It began with a phone call from a concerned father. His voice carried both frustration and worry as he spoke about the sudden changes he had noticed in his teenage daughter. Until recently, she had been a cheerful and talented child who actively participated in cultural activities. During her vacation, her parents enrolled her in a dance class, hoping she would enjoy her free time before entering Grade 10. Having won several prizes in the past, they believed it would be an opportunity for her to continue doing something she loved.

However, things did not go as expected. Within a short period, she lost interest in attending classes. Her dance teacher eventually informed the parents that it would be better if she stopped coming because she was no longer participating or showing enthusiasm. The family was puzzled. The same child who once loved singing and performing now seemed disconnected from the activities that had once brought her joy.

As the days passed, the changes became more noticeable. She preferred spending her time on her mobile phone and showed little interest in family conversations or social gatherings. Even when a close relative's wedding approached, she refused to attend despite repeated requests from her family. Her world seemed to be shrinking, with most of her attention focused on her online conversations.

Concerned by her behaviour, her father attempted to take away the phone, hoping it would help her reconnect with her surroundings. Instead, it led to arguments and resistance. Seeing her daughter's distress, the mother occasionally allowed her to use the phone without her father's knowledge. What began as an attempt to maintain peace at home only deepened the problem. The late-night phone usage increased, and the child often stayed awake chatting for hours. She would quietly take her mother's phone after everyone had gone to bed, spending long periods online when she believed nobody was watching.

The situation eventually reached a point where both parents felt helpless. What worried them most was not the phone itself, but the realization that their daughter was slowly withdrawing from family life, hobbies, and personal interactions. Looking for answers, they reached out to our team for support.

When the family met with us, our first step was not to blame the child or focus solely on the device. Instead, we listened. Through patient conversations with both the parents and the child, we explored the reasons behind the behaviour and helped the family understand that technology-related issues often have emotional and social dimensions. Together, we worked on rebuilding communication, establishing healthier digital habits, and creating an environment where trust could replace conflict.

Over time, the family began to recognize that cyber safety is not only about protecting devices and accounts. It is also about protecting relationships, emotional wellbeing, and the healthy development of young minds in a connected world.

This case serves as a reminder that significant changes in behaviour should never be ignored. Behind every screen may be a story that requires understanding, guidance, and support. Through awareness, early intervention, and open conversations, families can navigate digital challenges together and help young people build a healthier relationship with technology.

What appeared to be a promising business opportunity for a tea wholesaler from Kottayam began with a simple phone call. The caller introduced himself as a soldier and expressed interest in purchasing 500 kilograms of tea for a military canteen. For any trader, such a large order would naturally be welcome news, and the conversation soon moved to WhatsApp to discuss pricing, delivery, and payment procedures.

At first glance, everything seemed legitimate. The WhatsApp profile displayed a photograph of a soldier in uniform, and the caller communicated professionally. To further establish credibility, he shared a GST number for billing purposes. When the merchant verified the details, he found that the number was linked to Army Headquarters, which strengthened his confidence in the transaction.

The discussions progressed smoothly. The supposed soldier informed the merchant that the tea would need to be delivered to an NCC canteen in Parampuzha and assured him that all formalities would be completed before the order was finalized. Convinced that he was dealing with a genuine representative of a military institution, the merchant began preparing for the transaction.

However, the conversation soon took an unexpected turn. The caller informed the merchant that before any supply could be made, he needed to register himself as an authorized dealer on a special Sainik website. A link was sent through WhatsApp, and the merchant was instructed to complete a registration process. To verify that future financial transactions would work without issues, he was also asked to make a small payment of ₹100 through the same link.

To many people, such a small amount may appear harmless. After all, what risk could possibly be associated with paying just ₹100? But this request was actually the beginning of a carefully planned cyber fraud attempt. The small payment was not about the money itself—it was designed to lure the victim into entering sensitive banking information on a fraudulent platform controlled by scammers.

Fortunately, the merchant became suspicious before proceeding further and sought guidance regarding the transaction. Upon closer examination, several warning signs became clear. The use of military identities, official-looking profile photographs, genuine-looking GST details, and urgent procedural requirements were all part of a social engineering tactic intended to gain the victim's trust and bypass their caution.

This case serves as a powerful reminder that cybercriminals often exploit the public's respect for institutions such as the military, government departments, and law enforcement agencies. By combining publicly available information with convincing stories, they create a false sense of authenticity that can deceive even experienced business professionals.

The incident highlights an important lesson for individuals and businesses alike: always verify unexpected requests independently, avoid clicking on unverified links, and never enter banking credentials or make payments through platforms that have not been authenticated through official channels. In today's digital world, trust should always be accompanied by verification.

For many people, receiving a call from someone claiming to be a police officer, government official, or law enforcement representative is enough to create immediate fear. Cybercriminals understand this very well, and they have turned that fear into one of the most successful fraud techniques of recent times—the Digital Arrest Scam.

The story begins with a retired government official who received what appeared to be an urgent call from authorities. The caller claimed that the victim's identity had been linked to a serious criminal investigation involving financial crimes and money laundering. The victim was informed that legal action, including arrest and asset seizure, could follow if immediate cooperation was not provided.

What followed was a carefully orchestrated campaign of psychological manipulation. The fraudsters created an atmosphere of panic and urgency, convincing the victim that the matter was confidential and that discussing it with family members or friends could worsen the situation. The victim was instructed to remain available on calls for extended periods and was subjected to continuous monitoring and intimidation through video calls and messages.

The scammers presented forged documents, fake case files, and fabricated official notices that appeared convincing at first glance. Every interaction was designed to make the victim believe that a genuine investigation was underway. Under immense pressure and fear of legal consequences, the victim followed the instructions provided by the fraudsters.

By the time the deception was uncovered, approximately ₹18 lakh had been transferred to accounts controlled by the criminals.

Unfortunately, this is not an isolated incident. Across the country, cybercrime investigators continue to receive complaints involving so-called digital arrests, with losses ranging from a few lakhs to several crores of rupees. Experts believe that many more victims remain silent due to embarrassment, fear, or concern about public perception.

One of the most alarming aspects of these scams is that the victims are often highly educated individuals, retired professionals, senior executives, business owners, and government officials. Cybercriminals do not rely on technical hacking alone; they exploit human emotions such as fear, trust, authority, and urgency. As a result, intelligence and professional experience alone do not guarantee protection.

The concept of a "digital arrest" itself is a fraud. No legitimate law enforcement agency will conduct an arrest through a video call, demand secrecy, force continuous online monitoring, or ask individuals to transfer money to verify innocence. Such requests are clear warning signs of a scam.

This case highlights an important lesson for everyone: whenever an unexpected call creates fear and demands immediate action, pause and verify. Contact the relevant organization through official channels, speak with trusted family members, and never transfer money based solely on instructions received over a phone or video call.

Cybercriminals succeed when fear overrides judgment. Awareness, verification, and calm decision-making remain the strongest defences against digital arrest scams. It started like any ordinary day for a 48-year-old man from Kozhikode. While browsing social media, he came across a video featuring Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman discussing the Union Budget. As often happens with social media algorithms, similar financial and investment-related content soon began appearing frequently on his Facebook feed.

Among the many videos, one in particular captured his attention. A well-known financial expert was explaining how certain stock investments could generate extraordinary returns in a short period. The promise of doubling his investment sounded attractive, and curiosity quickly turned into interest.

Wanting to learn more, he searched online for information about the stock mentioned in the video. His search led him to a website that claimed to help investors identify high-performing stocks and maximize profits. After clicking a link on the page, he was redirected to a WhatsApp investment community.

At first glance, everything appeared genuine. The group was highly active, with hundreds of participants discussing investments, profits, and market opportunities. Messages continuously appeared from individuals claiming they had invested large sums and received impressive returns. Several members even shared screenshots of account statements and trading profits as proof of their success.

As he observed these conversations, a sense of urgency began to develop. Seeing so many people apparently earning significant profits made him feel that he might be missing out on a valuable opportunity. The thought that others were making money while he remained on the sidelines became difficult to ignore.

Eventually, he introduced himself in the group with a simple message expressing his interest in investing. Within minutes, he began receiving direct messages from individuals presenting themselves as investment experts and financial advisors. They appeared professional, knowledgeable, and eager to help.

The so-called experts shared detailed stock charts, projected profit calculations, dividend forecasts, and investment strategies. Every piece of information seemed carefully designed to build confidence and create the impression of a legitimate investment platform. The more he interacted with them, the more convinced he became that he had discovered an exclusive opportunity unavailable to ordinary investors.

Encouraged by the apparent success stories and reassured by the guidance of these "experts," he decided to make his first investment.

What he did not realize was that the entire operation had been carefully engineered to influence his decision. The active group members, the profit screenshots, the expert advice, and the promises of extraordinary returns were all part of a sophisticated social engineering scheme designed to build trust and create fear of missing out.

Like many modern investment scams, the fraud did not begin with a demand for money. It began with carefully targeted content, psychological manipulation, and the illusion of credibility. By the time victims recognize the deception, they often have already transferred substantial amounts of money.

This case serves as a reminder that investment opportunities promising unusually high or guaranteed returns should always be approached with caution. Genuine financial advisors do not pressure individuals into quick decisions, and legitimate investments do not rely on WhatsApp groups filled with unverified testimonials and profit screenshots.

In the digital age, fraudsters no longer need to break into bank accounts. They simply convince people to trust them. Awareness, verification, and independent research remain the strongest safeguards against investment scams disguised as opportunities for financial success.

For many young people searching for additional income, the promise of earning money from home can be difficult to ignore. This was exactly the situation faced by a 24-year-old man who came across what appeared to be an attractive job opportunity on social media.

The opportunity arrived through Instagram when a friend shared an advertisement claiming that anyone could earn between ₹1,000 and ₹4,000 per day by working online for just a few hours. The offer seemed simple, flexible, and ideal for someone looking to earn extra income without leaving home.

Curious to know more, he clicked the link attached to the advertisement. The details sounded convincing. According to the advertisement, participants would receive tasks every day and earn money immediately after completing them. Even if they could not complete all assigned tasks, they would still receive payment for the work already done. The advertisement emphasized flexibility, claiming that users could simply skip days when they were unavailable.

Interested by the possibility of easy earnings, the young man contacted the number mentioned in the advertisement. Shortly afterward, he was added to a Telegram group filled with messages from people who appeared to be successfully earning money through the platform. Many members shared screenshots of payments and claimed to be earning thousands of rupees every day.

As he observed the conversations, his confidence grew. The testimonials, payment screenshots, and positive discussions created the impression of a thriving online community. Believing the opportunity was genuine, he posted a message expressing his willingness to join the program.

Almost immediately, he received a direct message from an individual identifying themselves as an "HR Manager." The person provided detailed information about the role and explained how the tasks worked. The assignments appeared simple and required no special skills. Participants were asked to like social media profiles, engage with posts, leave comments, and perform other basic online activities.

Everything seemed legitimate. The tasks were easy, the communication was professional, and the promised earnings appeared achievable. However, what seemed like a straightforward online job was actually the beginning of a carefully planned scam.

Like many task-based fraud schemes operating today, the scammers first focus on building trust. Victims are shown proof of earnings, welcomed into active groups, and given small tasks to create confidence. Once trust is established, they are often encouraged to make deposits, purchase premium tasks, or invest money to unlock higher earnings. By the time victims realize the truth, they may have already transferred significant amounts of money.

This case highlights how cybercriminals exploit people's desire for financial security and flexible work opportunities. The promise of quick earnings, combined with social proof from fake testimonials and group discussions, can make fraudulent schemes appear convincing.

The lesson is simple but important: legitimate employers do not require payments to secure jobs, and genuine earning opportunities do not rely on Telegram groups filled with unverified success stories. Before joining any online work platform, always verify the company's credentials, research independently, and be cautious of opportunities that promise unusually high returns for minimal effort.

In the digital world, if an offer sounds too good to be true, it usually is. Awareness and careful verification remain the best defence against online job and task scams.

The story began with a young engineer from Thiruvananthapuram who, like thousands of professionals, turned to LinkedIn in search of better career opportunities. Every day, his feed was filled with job notifications from various companies. While he applied to some positions, many failed to meet his expectations regarding salary, location, or career growth.

One day, a particular advertisement caught his attention.

The post was published by what appeared to be a reputable HR consulting firm claiming partnerships with more than a hundred companies, including several international organizations. The vacancy was for an engineering position with a multinational company. The offer seemed almost perfect—an attractive salary package, a preferred location in Thiruvananthapuram, and even the flexibility to work from home for part of the week.

For any job seeker, the opportunity appeared difficult to ignore.

Interested in learning more, the engineer clicked the link attached to the advertisement. After reviewing the job description, he was directed to register for an interview. The registration process was straightforward, requiring him to fill out a form and pay a registration fee of ₹1,000.

Immediately after making the payment, he received confirmation messages through email and phone. Everything appeared professional and well-organized. He was informed that the interview schedule would be shared shortly.

A week later, an email arrived with the interview date and instructions. The message also included a link to download a specialized application supposedly required for participating in the company's video interview process. To make the process appear even more legitimate, the organizers provided a demo call facility that allowed candidates to test the platform before the interview.

On the scheduled day, the interview took place through a video call. Representatives claiming to be from the multinational company conducted a professional-looking interview and evaluated his qualifications and experience. At the end of the session, they congratulated him and informed him that he had successfully cleared the selection process.

Excited by the news, the young engineer listened carefully as the recruiters explained the next steps. Since the role involved potential international assignments, he was asked to provide documents including a copy of his passport and other personal credentials for processing. Soon afterward, he received an email from the HR department requesting soft copies of the required documents.

Believing he had secured the position, he submitted everything requested.

A few days later, another email arrived stating that visa processing and related documentation would require a payment of ₹10,000. Seeing this as a normal part of an international recruitment process, he transferred the amount and waited for his appointment letter.

Days turned into weeks.

Despite repeated follow-ups, no appointment letter arrived. Emails went unanswered, and communication gradually stopped. The HR representatives who had once been highly responsive could no longer be reached. As suspicion grew, he returned to LinkedIn to verify the original advertisement, only to discover that the HR company's profile and job posting had disappeared.

After waiting for nearly two months, he finally realized that the opportunity had never existed. The entire recruitment process—from the advertisement and registration fee to the interview and document collection—had been carefully designed to gain his trust and extract money and personal information.

The young engineer eventually approached the police and filed a complaint.

This case highlights a growing trend in cybercrime where fraudsters exploit the hopes and ambitions of job seekers. By creating convincing advertisements, conducting fake interviews, and mimicking legitimate recruitment procedures, scammers are able to deceive even educated and experienced professionals.

The lesson is clear: legitimate employers do not demand payments for recruitment, interviews, visa processing, or job confirmation. Before sharing personal documents or transferring money, candidates should independently verify the organization, contact the company through official channels, and carefully review the authenticity of job postings.

In today's digital world, not every opportunity is what it seems. Sometimes, the perfect job offer is simply the beginning of a carefully planned scam.

A few days ago, a businesswoman from Kochi received a Telegram message from an unknown number. The message contained a blurred image along with a simple question: "Is this you?"

Curious and alarmed, she opened the attachment. What she saw left her shocked. The image appeared to be a nude video featuring her face. Before she had time to process what had happened, another message arrived. The sender demanded ₹25,000 and threatened to share the video with her husband if the payment was not made immediately.

Fear took over.

Believing her reputation and family life were at risk, she transferred the money. But the payment did not stop the threats. More videos followed. New demands arrived. Over the next three days, she continued sending money in the hope that the harassment would end. By the time she approached the police, she had lost more than ₹40 lakh to cybercriminals who were using AI-generated fake videos to extort money.

This was not an isolated incident.

In another case from Kozhikode, a Plus One student received an image showing her apparently kissing a boy. The sender threatened to distribute the image among her classmates unless she immediately transferred money. Frightened and embarrassed, the student secretly used her mother's account to make the payment. When the transaction was discovered, her family initially misunderstood the situation and questioned her character rather than realizing she had become the victim of cybercrime.

A similar incident was reported from Kollam. A young woman preparing for marriage suddenly found herself at the centre of suspicion when manipulated images were sent to her fiancé. Despite having no connection to the individuals or locations shown in the images, she was forced to defend herself against allegations created entirely through digital manipulation.

Although the victims came from different backgrounds and faced different consequences, all three incidents shared a common factor. None of them had ever created or shared the images being used against them.

The fraud began with artificial intelligence.

Cybercriminals collected publicly available photographs from social media profiles, messaging applications, and other online sources. Using AI-powered deepfake technology, they created fake nude images and videos that appeared convincing enough to cause panic. The victims' fear became the scammers' most effective weapon.

In many cases, the attack started with a simple message designed to trigger curiosity and shock. Once victims clicked on malicious links, fraudsters attempted to gain access to personal information, photographs, contact lists, and other sensitive data stored on their devices. The criminals then used this information to create additional threats involving family members, friends, and colleagues.

Many victims believed that paying the demanded amount would end the harassment. Instead, each payment often led to further demands. As the scammers learned more about their victims' financial situations, the amounts increased. In some reported cases, victims lost tens of lakhs of rupees before seeking help.

What makes these scams particularly dangerous is that they target emotions rather than technology. Fear, embarrassment, panic, and concern for loved ones often prevent victims from thinking clearly. Cybercriminals understand this and exploit it ruthlessly.

This case highlights an important lesson for everyone. A photograph, video, or message appearing on a screen is no longer proof that it is genuine. Advances in artificial intelligence have made it possible to create highly realistic fake content capable of deceiving even careful observers.

If you receive threatening messages involving manipulated images or videos, do not panic, do not make payments, and do not negotiate with the sender. Preserve evidence, report the incident immediately, and seek assistance from cybercrime authorities.

In the age of artificial intelligence, protecting your digital identity has become just as important as protecting your financial assets. Awareness remains the strongest defence against the growing threat of deepfake-enabled cybercrime.

Several years ago, a family in Kozhikode learned a painful lesson about the dangers of sharing too much information online.

The family's teenage son was active on social media and enjoyed documenting almost every aspect of his daily life. Like many young content creators, he regularly shared videos, updates, and behind-the-scenes glimpses of life at home. To him, these posts were harmless. To someone watching with criminal intentions, they were valuable intelligence.

One day, the boy uploaded a video complaining that the air conditioner in his room had stopped working and that the heat inside the house had become unbearable. It seemed like an ordinary post that would quickly disappear among countless other social media updates.

The following day, two men arrived at the family's residence claiming to be technicians sent to repair the faulty air conditioner. At the time, only the elderly parents of the businessman who owned the house were present. Believing the visitors were legitimate service personnel, they allowed them inside.

The men inspected the air conditioner, moved around the room, and appeared to carry out repair work. After spending considerable time inside the house, they informed the family that the unit required replacement and that another technician would return later. They then left the premises.

Nothing appeared unusual.

However, when other family members returned home later that evening, they discovered that gold ornaments, cash, and mobile phones had disappeared from storage cupboards inside the house. The visitors had never come to repair the air conditioner. They had entered the home to steal.

The police investigation revealed something even more disturbing. The criminals had allegedly installed surveillance devices inside parts of the house. Investigators suspected that the intruders intended to monitor the family's movements and possibly gather information that could later be used to target the family's business operations as well.

One question continued to puzzle the family.

How did complete strangers know that the air conditioner was malfunctioning?

The answer was hidden in a social media post.

The information had been voluntarily shared online by a family member, unknowingly providing criminals with a believable reason to gain access to the house. What appeared to be an innocent video had become a roadmap for a planned crime.

This incident highlights a growing concern known as "sharenting" and excessive social media sharing. Parents and children often post photos, videos, home tours, vacation plans, school activities, and personal routines without considering how that information might be misused.

Every image shared online can reveal details about family members, daily schedules, valuables, locations, and security arrangements. Criminals no longer need to gather information through direct observation when people willingly publish it themselves.

Children are particularly vulnerable. During the rise of social media content creation, many young users began sharing videos showing their homes, bedrooms, travel plans, school routines, and family activities. While these videos may attract views and engagement, they can also expose sensitive information to strangers whose intentions are unknown.

What makes these risks more dangerous is that the audience is invisible. A child may believe they are sharing content with friends and followers, while in reality, anyone—including criminals—can access and analyse the information being posted.

Promoting children's talents and achievements online is not inherently wrong. However, sharing detailed information about family routines, property layouts, travel schedules, financial status, or household vulnerabilities can create serious security risks for everyone at home.

This case serves as a reminder that privacy is not only about protecting personal information—it is also about protecting physical safety. Before posting a photo, video, or update online, it is worth asking a simple question: Could this information be useful to someone with bad intentions?

In the digital age, a social media post can travel far beyond its intended audience. What seems like a harmless glimpse into daily life may reveal more than we realize. Responsible sharing, especially when children are involved, remains one of the most effective ways to protect both privacy and security.

The dangers of loan app scams once again came into public discussion following reports of victims facing severe harassment and emotional distress due to digital lending platforms. While many people believe that the problem begins when borrowers fail to repay loans on time, the reality is often much more complicated and dangerous.

This story is about Geetha, a homemaker from Kozhikode who was struggling to support her family during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Before the lockdown, Geetha earned a modest income through small jobs associated with a local community group. When the pandemic disrupted daily life and work opportunities disappeared, her family's financial situation became increasingly difficult. Medical expenses, household needs, and a lack of income placed enormous pressure on the family.

Like many others during that period, Geetha began using digital payment applications and online services for the first time. It was while browsing online that she came across an advertisement that seemed to offer exactly what she needed.

The advertisement promised instant, interest-free loans with minimal documentation. According to the offer, borrowers could access funds quickly and repay them through small monthly installments. The process appeared simple, convenient, and designed for people facing temporary financial difficulties.

Believing it could help her family overcome a difficult period, Geetha expressed interest in obtaining a loan of ₹3,000.

Soon afterward, she received a link directing her to download a loan application. The process was remarkably easy. She was asked to upload copies of her identification documents and complete a quick verification procedure. Unlike traditional lenders, there were no lengthy approval processes or credit score checks.

Within a short time, the loan was approved.

However, when the money arrived, Geetha noticed that the full amount had not been credited to her account. Various charges and service fees had been deducted before the transfer. Even so, she accepted the arrangement, believing she would repay the amount as soon as work resumed after the lockdown.

What happened next was completely unexpected.

Within days of receiving the loan, repayment reminders began arriving. Before Geetha could understand the schedule properly, she received messages claiming that her repayment had already been delayed. Additional penalties were added immediately.

Confused by the sudden demands, she contacted the number provided by the company and tried to explain that there must be a mistake. Instead of helping her, the representatives responded with screenshots and documents that allegedly showed she had agreed to entirely different loan terms.

As the conversation continued, the situation became more threatening.

The messages changed from repayment reminders to intimidation. The scammers claimed that they had access to personal information and photographs stored on her device. Soon afterward, they sent manipulated and inappropriate images and threatened to distribute them to her husband, relatives, and contacts if she failed to make immediate payments.

The loan that had started as a promise of financial relief had become a tool for harassment and extortion.

What Geetha did not realize when installing the application was that many fraudulent loan apps request extensive permissions that allow them to access contacts, photographs, messages, and other personal information stored on a phone. Once this information is collected, it can be used to pressure victims into making repeated payments.

For many victims, the financial loss is only part of the damage. The emotional stress, fear of public humiliation, and constant threats often create severe psychological pressure. In some cases, victims continue paying simply to prevent private information from being misused.

Geetha's experience serves as a powerful reminder that not all digital lending platforms are legitimate. Fraudulent loan apps often target people who are facing urgent financial needs, knowing that desperation can make risky offers appear trustworthy.

Before downloading any financial application, it is important to verify whether the lender is authorized, understand all loan terms carefully, and avoid applications that request unnecessary access to personal data. Most importantly, no legitimate lender will threaten borrowers with public humiliation, blackmail, or the misuse of private information.

What appeared to be a small loan meant to solve a temporary problem eventually became a digital trap. Awareness, caution, and verification remain the strongest protection against such scams in an increasingly connected world.
